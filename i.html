Rishikesh — The Yoga Capital of the World

"Rishikesh is not just a destination — it is a state of mind where the Ganges whispers secrets of the universe to those willing to listen."

Nestled in the foothills of the Himalayas where the sacred Ganges descends from the mountains onto the plains, Rishikesh is one of India's most spiritually vibrant cities. Known as the "Yoga Capital of the World," it draws seekers, adventurers, and pilgrims from every corner of the globe. The famous Beatles visited in 1968 to study Transcendental Meditation here, cementing its global reputation.

Mussoorie — The Queen of the Hills

"Like a string of pearls stretched across the lower Himalayan ridge, Mussoorie glitters against the mountains — charming, colonial, and captivating."

Perched at 2,000 metres in the Garhwal Himalayan range, Mussoorie has enchanted visitors since the British first built their summer retreats here in the early 1800s. Known as the "Queen of the Hills," it offers sweeping panoramic views of the Doon Valley below and the eternal snows above. The Mall Road still retains its colonial elegance, lined with grand old hotels, cheerful shops, and roadside cafés.

Nainital — The City of Lakes

"Nainital cradles a sapphire lake in a bowl of emerald hills — a gift of nature so perfect it seems designed by a divine artist."

Sitting in a natural crater at 2,084 metres, Nainital is centred around the Naini Lake — a gorgeous freshwater lake shaped like an eye (Naini = eye). The town cascades down the hillsides around it in tiers of colourful buildings, hotels and bazaars. This beloved Kumaon hill station was once the summer capital of the United Provinces during British rule and continues to be one of Uttarakhand's most visited destinations.

✦ Sacred Shrine

Kedarnath — Abode of Lord Shiva

"To reach Kedarnath is to leave the world behind. At 3,583 metres, the only thing between you and the eternal is the ancient stone of Shiva's shrine."

Kedarnath is one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva — and undeniably the most dramatically situated. The ancient stone temple, believed to be over 1,200 years old and credited to Adi Shankaracharya, sits beneath the magnificent Kedarnath peak. The 22 km trek from Gaurikund through rhododendron forests and alpine meadows is a pilgrimage of body and soul. The temple is open only for six months (May–November) as Kedarnath lies under heavy snow the rest of the year.

✦ UNESCO World Heritage

Valley of Flowers — Himalayan Eden

"In the monsoon months, the Valley of Flowers erupts in a riot of colour so vivid, so overwhelming, that it seems the mountains themselves have decided to celebrate."

Designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2005, the Valley of Flowers National Park is a spectacular high-altitude Himalayan meadow spread across 87 square kilometres. Located in the Chamoli district of Uttarakhand at heights ranging from 3,352m to 6,675m, the valley bursts into an astonishing carpet of over 500 species of wildflowers during the monsoon months of July to September. It was discovered by British mountaineer Frank Smythe in 1931.

✦ Char Dham

Badrinath — Seat of Lord Vishnu

"Badrinath — where the Alaknanda roars, where the Neelkanth peak towers in white, and where Lord Vishnu is believed to meditate in eternal solitude."

Situated at 3,133 metres in the Chamoli district, Badrinath is one of the most revered Hindu pilgrimage sites — a Dhaam dedicated to Lord Vishnu. The colourful temple facade, the sacred Tapt Kund hot spring, the Brahmakapal ghat for ancestor rituals, and the mighty Neelkanth peak (6,596m) as a backdrop make this a visually and spiritually overwhelming destination. Part of both the Char Dham and Panch Badri circuits.

✦ Ski Destination

Auli — The Ski Slopes of the Himalayas

"Auli offers what few ski destinations in the world can — the backdrop of Nanda Devi, Mana Parbat and Kamet while you carve through pristine powder snow."

Auli is India's premier ski resort, situated at 2,500–3,050 metres in Chamoli district. It has some of Asia's best ski slopes with 16 km of pisted runs and unobstructed views of some of the world's highest peaks including Nanda Devi (7,816m), India's second highest mountain. GMVN and private operators run ski courses for beginners through to advanced skiers, with equipment rental available. Auli also connects to the Kuari Pass trek — one of Uttarakhand's finest.

✦ Wildlife & Nature

Jim Corbett National Park — Land of the Tiger

"In Corbett, every rustle of grass, every alarm call of a deer, every shadow in the sal forest is a reminder that you are a guest in a world that belongs to the tiger."

Established in 1936 as India's first national park, Jim Corbett National Park is spread over 1,318 sq km of the Himalayan foothills in Nainital district. Named after the legendary hunter-turned-conservationist Jim Corbett, the park is one of India's finest tiger reserves with a population of over 260 Bengal tigers. The Ramganga river flows through the park, creating rich riparian habitats for wildlife. The park has five zones: Bijrani, Jhirna, Dhela, Durga Devi, and Dhikala — the most scenic of all.

✦ Trekking Paradise

Chopta & Tungnath — Uttarakhand's Mini Switzerland

"Chopta's meadows glow red in spring with a million rhododendron blooms. From Tungnath's ancient stones, the entire arc of the high Himalayas is spread before you like a living painting."

Chopta, nestled at 2,680 metres in the Rudraprayag district, is often called "Mini Switzerland" for its lush rolling meadows and unobstructed mountain views. The 3.5 km trek from Chopta leads to Tungnath — at 3,680m, the highest Shiva temple in the world, part of the Panch Kedar circuit. A further 1.5 km hike reaches Chandrashila peak at 4,130m, offering a full 360° Himalayan panorama including Nanda Devi, Trishul, Kedar Peak and Chaukhamba.

✦ Holy City

Haridwar — Gateway to the Gods

"At Har Ki Pauri, as the Ganga Aarti begins at dusk, thousands of oil lamps float on the sacred waters — and for a moment, the divine feels touchable."

Haridwar, literally meaning "Gateway to God," is where the sacred Ganges emerges from the Shivalik Hills onto the plains. It is one of the seven holiest places in Hinduism and hosts the Kumbh Mela — the world's largest religious gathering — every 12 years. The evening Ganga Aarti at Har Ki Pauri ghat is an experience that transcends religion — a cascade of fire, sound, and devotion that moves even the most sceptical visitor to their core.

✦ Offbeat Escape

Lansdowne — Untouched Hill Station

"Lansdowne is Uttarakhand's best-kept secret — a colonial cantonment town where the British-era silence has barely been disturbed, and the oak forests still belong to the woodpeckers."

Lansdowne is a small, unhurried cantonment hill station at 1,706m in Pauri Garhwal district, developed by the British in 1887 and named after then-Viceroy Lord Lansdowne. It remains the regimental headquarters of the Garhwal Rifles, one of India's most decorated infantry regiments. Unlike the crowded hill stations, Lansdowne has deliberately kept tourism minimal, preserving its colonial character — red-roofed buildings, well-kept gardens, and ancient oak and rhododendron forests.

✦ Food & Culture

Uttarakhandi Cuisine — Mountain Flavours

"Uttarakhandi cuisine is the food of the mountains — simple, nourishing, flavoured with local herbs and cooked over slow fire, carrying centuries of highland wisdom in every bite."

The cuisine of Uttarakhand reflects its terrain — hardy, wholesome, and deeply connected to local produce. With limited arable land in the mountains, traditional dishes were built around locally grown grains like mandua (finger millet), jhangora (barnyard millet), and gahat (horse gram) rather than rice and wheat. Mustard oil, ghee, and locally foraged greens form the base of most cooking. The food is split between Garhwali (western) and Kumaoni (eastern) traditions, each with its own distinct flavours.

Must-Try Dishes

  • Kafuli — creamy spinach and fenugreek curry, cooked in iron kadai
  • Chainsoo — roasted black gram dal tempered with local spices
  • Phanu — thick lentil soup slow-cooked over hours, Garhwali staple
  • Aloo ke Gutke — spiced potatoes with jakhiya seeds (endemic to the region)
  • Bal Mithai — Kumaoni fudge coated in white sugar pellets, a beloved sweet
  • Buransh Sharbat — juice made from rhododendron flowers, startlingly delicious
  • Mandua ki Roti — finger millet flatbread, nutritious and earthy in flavour

Garhwali Specialities

  • Kafuli (green curry)
  • Phanu (lentil soup)
  • Dubke (black bean curry)
  • Singori (leaf-wrapped sweet)

Kumaoni Specialities

  • Bal Mithai (fudge)
  • Alu Tamatar Jhol
  • Rus (mixed lentils)
  • Bhaang ki Chutney

✦ Char Dham

Gangotri & Yamunotri — Source of Sacred Rivers

"Where glaciers birth sacred rivers and ancient temples guard their cradles — Gangotri and Yamunotri are not merely pilgrimage sites; they are the origin stories of a civilization."

Gangotri and Yamunotri are two of the four sacred Char Dhams of Uttarakhand, representing the sources of India's holiest rivers. Yamunotri (3,293m) in Uttarkashi district is the source of the Yamuna River, where the Divya Shila sacred rock and Surya Kund hot spring await pilgrims after a 6 km trek from Janki Chatti. Gangotri (3,048m), also in Uttarkashi, is the origin of the Bhagirathi River (one of the main headstreams of the Ganges), flanked by the towering Bhagirathi peaks.

✦ Heritage & Traditions

Kumaon & Garhwal — Culture, Art & Festivals

"The folk culture of Uttarakhand is a living museum — Aipan floor art, Pahari miniature paintings, Bairagarh weaves, and the haunting melodies of the dhol-damau kept alive in every mountain village."

Uttarakhand's two distinct cultural regions — Kumaon in the east and Garhwal in the west — each carry their own unique artistic, musical, and festive traditions. The people of the mountains developed rich folk arts as expressions of faith, celebration, and identity in their isolated valleys. UNESCO has recognised several of these traditions, and there is a growing movement to preserve them.

Traditional Arts

  • Aipan — ritual floor art in red & white
  • Pahari miniature painting
  • Ringal bamboo craft
  • Woollen weaving — Pattu & Thulma
  • Copper & brass metalwork

Major Festivals

  • Kumbh Mela — Haridwar (every 12 yrs)
  • Phool Dei — spring flower festival
  • Harela — sowing season harvest fest
  • Ghee Sankranti — melted butter ritual
  • Nanda Devi Raj Jat Yatra (12-yearly)

Music & Dance

  • Jhora & Chholiya — Kumaoni folk dances performed at weddings and festivals
  • Langvir Nritya — acrobatic dance performed atop a bamboo pole
  • Dhol-Damau — traditional drum ensemble that defines festive processions
  • Jagar — shamanic ritual music to invoke mountain deities, still practised today
  • Bairagarh textile weaving — distinctive geometric patterns on handlooms

"In Uttarakhand, the divine lives not only in temples but in the patterns drawn on courtyard floors, in the songs sung at harvest, and in the faces of people who have learned to live with mountains."

— Ethnographic Studies, Kumaon University

✦ Remote Himalayan Village

Munsiyari — Little Kashmir of Kumaon

"Munsiyari sits at the foot of the Panchachuli — five snow peaks named for the five cooking fires of the Pandavas. The view from the bazaar at dawn is unforgettable."

Munsiyari, meaning "Place with Snow," is a small frontier town at 2,200m in Pithoragarh district, positioned at the confluence of the Johar, Gori and Dharma valleys near the Tibetan border. It is the base for some of Kumaon's most spectacular treks — Milam Glacier, Ralam Glacier, and Khaliya Top. The Panchachuli massif (five peaks, the highest at 6,904m) dominates the skyline and can be clearly seen from the town's apple orchards.

✦ Practical Guide

Travel Tips — Planning Your Uttarakhand Journey

"A journey to Uttarakhand requires not just a packed bag but a prepared mind — for the mountains demand patience, respect, and the willingness to slow down."

Best Season by Zone

  • Hill stations: Mar – Jun, Sep – Nov
  • Char Dham: May – June, Sept – Oct
  • Wildlife (Corbett): Nov – June
  • Skiing (Auli): Jan – March
  • Valley of Flowers: July – Sept

How to Reach

  • By Air: Jolly Grant (Dehradun), Pantnagar (Nainital area)
  • By Rail: Haridwar, Rishikesh, Kathgodam, Dehradun
  • By Road: NH-58 (Char Dham), NH-87 (Kumaon), NH-119 (Corbett)
  • Helicopter: GMVN operates Char Dham heli services

Essential Tips

  • Register for Char Dham Yatra online at registrationandtouristcare.uk.gov.in before travelling
  • Carry altitude sickness medication (Diamox) if going above 3,000m
  • Mountain roads can close in monsoon (July–Aug) — always check NHAI updates
  • Inner Line Permit required for areas near China border (Munsiyari's Milam, Nelong Valley)
  • Uttarakhand is vegetarian-friendly — most dhabas near temples are pure-veg
  • Respect wildlife and carry bags for your waste — Leave No Trace principles
  • Local STD code: Dehradun (0135), Nainital (05942), Haridwar (01334)
  • Tourist helpline: 1364 (Uttarakhand Tourism)
